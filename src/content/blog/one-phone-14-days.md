Before my first trip to China, a friend asked me, "How much cash should I bring?"
"I don't know," I said. "Maybe a few hundred pounds?"
She laughed. "You won't need it. Just your phone."

I didn't believe her. In London, I use my phone for some things, like contactless payments on the Tube and ordering takeaway. But cash and cards are still everywhere. You can't buy a newspaper from a street vendor with Apple Pay. You can't pay for a bus ticket with a wave of your phone if you're not on a specific route.

So I packed my wallet anyway. I even went to the bank to get crisp new notes.

But on my first morning in Shanghai, I decided to try an experiment. I left my wallet in the hotel safe. I took only my phone and a photocopy of my passport just in case.

I walked to a convenience store. Picked up a bottle of water and a rice ball. The cashier pointed at a QR code. I opened **Alipay (zhīfùbǎo)**, which I'd set up the night before using my foreign credit card. Scanned. The screen flashed "Payment successful: ¥9.80". That was it. No fumbling for coins, no signature, no PIN.

I felt a strange thrill. *This is how they live every day.*

Over the next two weeks, I pushed the experiment further.

I took a ride-hailing app (China's version of Uber) from my hotel to the Bund. The app charged me automatically. I didn't even have to press "pay". I ordered lunch from a dumpling shop through a food delivery app. Twenty minutes later, a scooter rider handed me a warm bag. I bought a high-speed train ticket from Shanghai to Hangzhou on my phone, with no ticket office, no queue, just a QR code on my screen.

By day four, I'd forgotten my wallet even existed.

The most surprising moment came in a small alley in Hangzhou. An old woman was roasting sweet potatoes in a charcoal drum. The smell was incredible. I asked how much. "Six yuan," she said. I pulled out my phone, expecting her to shake her head. Instead, she pointed to a faded QR code taped to the side of her cart. I scanned, paid with **Alipay (zhīfùbǎo)**, and walked away with a steaming sweet potato.

That's when it hit me: *this isn't just convenience. It's infrastructure.* From a street vendor to a five-star hotel, from a museum ticket booth to a hospital registration desk, everyone, everywhere, expects you to pay with your phone.

One evening, I tried to find a limit. I booked a hotel room, ordered dinner, bought a ticket for a light show, paid for a shared bike, and sent money to a friend, all on my phone, all within an hour. No card swiped. No cash handed over.

Later, when I needed to split dinner with new friends, I used **WeChat Pay (wēixìn zhīfù)**. They scanned my QR code, and the money appeared in my account instantly. No awkward "I'll get it next time", just seamless digital trust.

Back in London, after my trip, I went to a café. I automatically reached for my phone to pay. The cashier looked at me strangely. "Card or cash only," she said. I fumbled for my wallet. It felt clumsy, slow, old-fashioned.

I realised then: China hadn't just been a trip. It had reset my default.
