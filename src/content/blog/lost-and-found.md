I arrived in China with a list of warnings from back home:
"Never leave your bag unattended."
"Keep your phone in your front pocket."
"Don't trust strangers too easily."

So on my first morning in Shanghai, when I walked into a Starbucks and saw laptops, phones, and designer bags sitting alone on tables while their owners queued for coffee, I froze.
Where are the security guards?
Why isn't anyone watching their stuff?

My friend Lucy, who had lived in China for two years, just laughed.
"People don't steal here like they do in London," she said. "Just put your bag down, go order, and come back. Nothing will happen."

I didn't believe her.
So I did a little experiment. I left my phone on the table, my open backpack beside it, and walked to the counter. My heart pounded. I kept glancing over my shoulder.

By the time I got my latte, everything was still there. I tried again the next day, left my laptop this time. Same result.

It felt unreal. In London, that laptop would have been gone in sixty seconds.

This "trust as default" thing started to sink in. I stopped clutching my bag like a nervous cat. I began to enjoy the freedom of not having to guard every little thing.

And then, in Chengdu, the real test came.

I was at a popular scenic spot, taking photos of the old teahouses. It was crowded. I got distracted by a street performer, put my backpack down on a bench to get a better angle, and walked off. Fifteen minutes later, I realised my bag was gone.

Inside: my passport, my wallet, my power bank, and worst of all, a small hard drive with all my travel photos and journal drafts.

My heart sank. For a moment, the old fear crept back. *This is it. This is what they warned me about.*

I rushed to a nearby police booth, barely able to explain myself. The officer spoke a little English. He asked calmly: "Where? When? What colour bag?"

He pulled up a tablet and tapped a few times. Within minutes, he was scrolling through security footage from a camera on a lamppost. There I was, putting the bag down. Then me walking away. Then a minute later, an elderly woman picking it up and walking towards a ticket office.

"Don't worry," the officer said. "She probably turned it in. That's what people do here."

We walked to the ticket office. And sure enough, there was my backpack, sitting behind the counter. A young woman handed it to me. "We were waiting for you," she said. "We saw you leave it. We knew you'd come back."

Everything was inside. Nothing missing. Not even the loose change.

I cried a little. Not from relief, from something bigger. In my country, if you lose your bag in a crowded tourist spot, it's gone. Forever. The police might file a report, but they wouldn't sit with you and scroll through cameras within minutes. And a stranger? Most would not go out of their way to turn it in.

That night, I sat in a quiet hostel room and thought about what I'd learned.
China isn't just a place where people leave laptops in cafes. It's a place where even when things go wrong, the system and the people work together to make it right.

The cameras I'd heard about? They're not watching you. They're watching out for you.
The trust I'd tested in Starbucks? It's not blind. It's backed by something real.

I wrote in my journal that night:
"Trust is the default here. And when that fails, the village still has your back."

That's the China I now carry with me, not just the pandas and the skyscrapers, but the quiet, solid feeling that you're never really alone.
